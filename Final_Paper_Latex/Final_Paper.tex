\section{Introduction}
This document provides a rigorous evaluation of the \textbf{Fama-French 5-Factor Model} using R. The model extends the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) by incorporating Size, Value, Profitability, and Investment factors alongside standard market risk \parencite{fama1993common, fama2015five}. The core objective is to determine the efficacy of these five factors in explaining the excess returns of a portfolio comprising seven selected assets.
The shift from the single-factor CAPM was driven by its poor empirical record, which, as noted by \textcite{fama2004capital}, is often considered insufficient for practical applications. While the CAPM posits that risk is measured solely relative to a comprehensive market portfolio, empirical tests frequently show that the relation between average return and beta is flatter than predicted. This gap between theory and evidence necessitated a multidimensional approach to capture the risk factors that a single market beta could not account for.

\subsection{Transition from CAPM to Fama-French 5-Factor Model}
The Fama-French Model, developed by \textcite{fama1992cross} in their paper titled ‘The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns’, was originally composed of three (3) factors and serves as an augmentation to the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). The primary purpose of the introduction of this model was to demonstrate that market risk alone cannot fully explain stock returns. Thus, the transition from a single-factor (beta) linear model (CAPM) to a multi-factor linear model led to the creation of a model that could provide a multidimensional assessment for expected returns. Here, the CAPM model was expanded to include firm-specific variables that are historically known to have an effect on returns. \\

The initial Fama-French Model was composed of the following systematic risk factors. First, Market Risk (Rf), taken from the original CAPM model, represents the excess returns of the overall market over the overall risk-free rate. Next, SMB (small minus big) represents the idea that small-cap stocks tend to outperform large-cap stocks over time. Lastly, there is HML (high minus low) or the value factor; stocks with higher BM ratios tend to outperform stocks with low BM ratios. The three-factor model was originally developed to encapsulate the relationship between average return and price ratios (such as book-to-market ratio) \parencite{fama1993common}. \\

Moving forward, the Fama-French 3-Factor Model was further augmented by Fama and French in 2015 in their working paper titled ‘A five-factor asset pricing model’. This model is composed of the three factors mentioned above with the further additions of RMW (robust minus weak) and CMA (conservative minus aggressive). The factor of RMW represents the difference between the returns of stocks with robust and weak profitability. Meanwhile, CMA represents the difference between the returns on diversified portfolios of stocks of low and high investment firms, which are referred to as conservative and aggressive \parencite{fama2015five}. These labels refer to a company’s investment policy, whether it can be considered conservative or aggressive \parencite{MOSOEU202255}. This new model with these two newly added variables is what is now referred to as the Fama-French 5-Factor Model. \\


\subsection{Theoretical Significance and Professional Relevance}
The 5-factor model holds immense practical value for finance professionals, particularly in the realms of portfolio evaluation and cost of capital estimation. By identifying specific anomalies—such as the size and value effects—the model allows fund managers to more accurately assess whether a portfolio's "alpha" is the result of genuine skill or simply exposure to known risk premiums. 

Furthermore, the model's ability to provide a more nuanced risk assessment assists analysts in estimating the cost of equity more reliably than the traditional CAPM. Understanding these factors is vital for asset pricing, as it helps investors recognize that expected returns are tied not just to market volatility, but to the underlying characteristics of the firms themselves, such as their profitability and investment policies \parencite{fama2015five}.

\subsection{Portfolio Management}
The Five-Factor Model redefines risk beyond market risk or beta from the CAPM model. \parencite{fama2015five} state that profitability and investment policy are closely linked to expected returns, as they are directly linked to a firm’s cash flows and investment behavior. Firms with weak operational profitability have low expected returns, similarly to firms that have an aggressive investment policy. Thus, these factors become systematic risks. The Five-Factor Model identifies these risk exposures explicitly, allowing financial managers to understand fundamental risk structure.

\subsection{Asset Pricing}
This model captures the most variation in average returns that were previously unexplained by the CAPM model and the three-factor model. In several tests, the intercepts are substantially lower than the three-factor model, thus indicating better pricing performance. Moreover, the cost of equity calculations can incorporate firm profitability and investment exposure. Thus, serving as a stronger benchmark for finding anomalies and evaluating corporate asset pricing policies.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Research Design and Justification}
This study employs a quantitative research approach, utilizing an empirical case study design to evaluate the explanatory power of the Fama-French 5-Factor Model. The research design is cross-sectional and longitudinal, focusing on the statistical relationship between historical asset returns and established risk premiums over a defined temporal horizon. By using multivariate linear regression, the study aims to objectively measure factor loadings and assess their significance in a reproducible environment.

The choice of a multivariate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression framework is justified by asset pricing theory, which posits that the cross-section of expected returns can be explained by shared risk factors. This method is the most appropriate for this study because it allows for the decomposition of returns into specific risk premiums (Market, SMB, HML, RMW, and CMA), providing a granular view of an asset's risk profile that a single-factor model like the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) cannot achieve. Furthermore, using R and its specialized financial libraries ensures computational accuracy and the ability to handle high-frequency data transformations effectively.

\subsection{Technical Workflow and Data Integration}
The methodology follows a systematic, reproducible workflow designed to align historical asset returns with the Fama-French risk premiums. The process is divided into data acquisition, preprocessing, and econometric modeling stages, ensuring that the results are statistically robust and grounded in established financial theory. All data ingestion and analysis are managed within an RMarkdown (\texttt{.Rmd}) environment to maintain a transparent and auditable trail of the data collection process.

\subsubsection{Data Acquisition and Instruments}
Data collection is conducted through automated, secondary sources utilizing specialized software instruments within a self-contained R environment. Two primary data streams are utilized for this analysis. Factor risk premiums—comprising the independent variables (Market, SMB, HML, RMW, and CMA) and the risk-free rate ($R_f$)—are sourced from the Kenneth R. French Data Library, specifically utilizing the ``U.S. Research Returns'' archive to maintain consistency with historical benchmarks. 

Asset return data, consisting of historical adjusted closing prices for the seven selected assets, are retrieved via the \texttt{tidyquant} and \texttt{quantmod} R packages. To ensure workflow efficiency and reproducibility, the technical implementation employs a conditional data persistence strategy: the script automatically verifies the presence of local \texttt{.csv} files in the \texttt{data/} directory before initiating remote calls to the Yahoo Finance API. This dual-layered approach ensures data consistency across the research team while minimizing API latency and redundant network requests.

\subsubsection{Data Analysis and Preprocessing Procedures}
A critical component of the technical implementation is the synchronization of temporal indices. Since asset data and factor data often utilize different date formats, all indices are normalized to a standard YYYY-MM-01 format to ensure a perfect merge. Following standard asset pricing theory, raw price data is converted into monthly log-returns to facilitate linear regression modeling and address potential non-linearity in return data. Excess returns for each asset are calculated by subtracting the monthly risk-free rate ($R_f$) from the raw monthly returns ($R_i - R_f$). Furthermore, in accordance with the Kenneth French Library protocols, any missing values (typically coded as -99.99 or -99.9) are identified and handled to prevent skewing the regression results.

\subsubsection{Multivariate Regression Framework}
The core analytics are performed using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression within the R environment. To ensure the validity of this framework, a correlation analysis of the five independent variables was conducted. Table \ref{tab:factor_corr} confirms that the risk premiums exhibit low pairwise correlations, satisfying the OLS assumption of no perfect multicollinearity and allowing for the isolation of specific factor effects.

We estimate the following 5-factor equation for each of our seven selected assets:
\begin{equation}
R_{it} - R_{ft} = \alpha_i + \beta_{i1}(R_{Mt} - R_{ft}) + \beta_{i2}SMB_t + \beta_{i3}HML_t + \beta_{i4}RMW_t + \beta_{i5}CMA_t + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
This model allows us to decompose the source of returns for our portfolio, identifying whether performance is driven by market risk, size, value, profitability, or investment intensity. In addition to the regression coefficients, key descriptive statistics—including Mean, Median, Standard Deviation, Skewness, and Kurtosis—are calculated to analyze the distributional characteristics of the returns.

The ``population'' consists of the universe of globally traded exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and equities. The sample was deliberately selected to represent specific factor tilts toward Market, SMB, HML, RMW, and CMA factors, providing a comprehensive dataset for evaluating the model's explanatory power across different asset classes and investment styles. To capture the specific risk premiums identified by the Fama-French framework, the portfolio includes a selection of seven exchange-traded funds (ETFs) with distinct factor tilts. To incorporate regional tax optimization within a 2026 investment framework, the portfolio utilizes London-listed UCITS variants of the target assets. These assets maintain direct physical exposure to the underlying US factors while optimizing for withholding tax efficiencies, thereby testing the Fama-French model's robustness across cross-border listing environments. The baseline for market exposure is established through \textbf{VUSD.L} (Vanguard S\&P 500 UCITS), which serves as the primary proxy for broad U.S. equity market beta ($R_i - R_f$). This is complemented by \textbf{VWRA.L} (Vanguard FTSE All-World), representing the global market portfolio and ensuring the model accounts for aggregate risk across both developed and emerging regions. To further refine geographical exposure, \textbf{VDEV.L} (Vanguard FTSE Developed World) is utilized to test the model's validity across the broader developed-market universe beyond the United States.

Factor-specific tilts are achieved through targeted allocations. The size premium is captured via \textbf{WSML.L} (iShares MSCI World Small Cap), which holds a diversified set of global small-capitalization firms hypothesized to outperform large-cap counterparts over time. The profitability (RMW) factor is represented by \textbf{QUAL} (iShares MSCI USA Quality Factor), which filters for companies with high operating profitability and stable earnings. Conversely, the investment (CMA) factor is addressed through \textbf{VIG} (Vanguard Dividend Appreciation); firms with consistent dividend increases typically follow conservative reinvestment policies, prioritizing shareholder returns over aggressive expansion.

To provide a counter-balance to value-oriented factors, \textbf{XLK} (Technology Select Sector SPDR) introduces a significant growth tilt, characterized by lower book-to-market ratios. Finally, \textbf{IAU} (iShares Gold Trust) is included as a non-equity stabilizer. While gold does not carry direct exposure to standard Fama-French equity premiums, its inclusion serves a critical diagnostic purpose: it allows for an evaluation of the model's intercept behavior (Alpha) when applied to assets with zero hypothesized factor loadings.

\subsubsection{High Explanatory Power: Diversified Equity Funds}

The Fama-French 5-Factor Model demonstrates exceptional explanatory capacity across all seven equity-oriented assets in the portfolio. QUAL achieves the highest Adjusted $R^2$ of 0.974, meaning that the five risk premiums collectively account for 97.4\% of the variation in its monthly excess returns---a near-exhaustive systematic attribution. VUSD.L and VDEV.L follow closely with Adjusted $R^2$ values of 0.948 and 0.947 respectively, confirming that the return dynamics of broad U.S. and developed-market equity exposure are almost entirely captured by the five-factor structure. WSML.L and VWRA.L achieve comparable Adjusted $R^2$ values of 0.940 and 0.939, validating that both the global small-cap and all-world equity mandates remain well within the explanatory domain of the framework. VIG and XLK, while marginally lower, still achieve Adjusted $R^2$ values of 0.926 and 0.903 respectively. In every equity case the overall $F$-test yields a model $p$-value well below 0.001, providing decisive evidence that the five-factor structure is jointly significant in explaining excess returns. These results confirm that, once the contaminating effect of currency basis is eliminated through USD-denomination alignment, the Fama-French framework constitutes a highly accurate and sufficient risk attribution tool for the equity core of this portfolio.

\subsubsection{Low Explanatory Power: Gold as a Diagnostic Asset}

IAU (iShares Gold Trust) registers a strikingly low Adjusted $R^2$ of 0.068, and the overall model $F$-test is statistically insignificant at the 5\% level ($p = 0.102$). This outcome decisively confirms the diagnostic hypothesis that motivated its inclusion: physical gold returns are structurally independent of the systematic equity risk factors. The stark contrast between IAU's near-zero model fit and the uniformly high explanatory power observed across the seven equity funds validates the model's correct specification---the framework captures equity-specific systematic risk, not asset returns in general. As a further diagnostic, it empirically demonstrates that the strong Adjusted $R^2$ values obtained for the equity assets are not the product of overfitting or model misspecification, but rather reflect genuine systematic factor exposures.

\subsubsection{Alpha Interpretation}

The regression intercepts---the alpha ($\hat{\alpha}$) coefficients---represent the portion of excess returns not attributable to any of the five systematic risk factors, and their magnitude and significance carry important economic implications. Statistically significant negative alphas were identified for QUAL ($\hat{\alpha} = -0.0029$, $p < 0.05$) and VWRA.L ($\hat{\alpha} = -0.0030$, $p < 0.05$). These negative intercepts are most plausibly attributed to the real-world friction costs of investing in exchange-traded products---specifically management expense ratios and transaction costs---which the theoretical Fama-French factor portfolios, being constructed as paper accounts, do not bear. By contrast, VUSD.L exhibited an intercept indistinguishable from zero ($\hat{\alpha} = 0.0000$), while XLK's positive alpha of 0.0019 was also statistically insignificant, suggesting that for both funds, the estimated factor loadings fully account for observed returns. IAU's positive but statistically insignificant alpha ($\hat{\alpha} = 0.0035$, $p > 0.05$) further reinforces the interpretation that gold's return-generating process falls outside the explanatory domain of the five-factor equity model.

\textbf{Investment Factor (CMA).} Statistically significant CMA loadings are recorded for VDEV.L ($0.1680$, $p < 0.05$), VWRA.L ($0.1722$, $p < 0.05$), and VIG ($0.1940$, $p < 0.05$). The positive sign indicates that firms within the Developed World, All-World, and dividend-appreciation universes systematically employ more conservative reinvestment strategies relative to the broad market portfolio---a finding consistent with the theoretical linkage between dividend-paying behavior and conservative investment policy. The CMA loadings for QUAL, VUSD.L, WSML.L, and XLK are small and statistically insignificant, suggesting no systematic tilt toward either conservative or aggressive investment policies within those specific mandates.

\subsection{Cross-Asset Comparison and Portfolio Diagnostics}

Taken collectively, the regression results confirm that the eight-asset portfolio exhibits well-defined and theoretically coherent factor profiles across all five Fama-French dimensions. A consistent interpretation emerges from both the model fitness statistics and the estimated coefficients: the portfolio can be organized into two analytically distinct categories based on the degree to which the Fama-French framework explains return variation.

The \textbf{first category} comprises the seven equity-oriented funds: QUAL, VUSD.L, VDEV.L, WSML.L, VWRA.L, VIG, and XLK. For these assets, the model operates at near-maximum explanatory capacity, with Adjusted $R^2$ values uniformly exceeding 0.90. The estimated factor loadings align closely with the theoretical tilts embedded in each fund's investment mandate---XLK's high market beta and significantly negative HML loading confirm its growth orientation; QUAL and VIG's robust positive RMW loadings validate their quality and profitability mandates; WSML.L's dominant positive SMB loading unambiguously identifies its small-capitalization exposure; and the positive CMA loadings of VDEV.L, VWRA.L, and VIG point toward systematically conservative reinvestment behavior within their respective index universes. An important caveat within this equity category concerns the divergence between factor exposure and factor reward. WSML.L's large and statistically significant SMB loading confirms that it carries substantial and genuine small-cap risk; however, as Figure~\ref{fig:cum_returns} illustrates, this exposure generated comparatively modest cumulative returns over the 2015--2026 period. This underperformance relative to other equity ETFs reflects the time-varying nature of factor risk premiums and serves as an empirical reminder that factor loading and factor reward are not synonymous: exposure to a risk premium does not guarantee its realization within any given sample period.

The \textbf{second category} consists solely of IAU (iShares Gold Trust), for which the model's overall $F$-test is statistically insignificant ($p = 0.102$) and the Adjusted $R^2$ of 0.068 is negligible. This outcome is not a failure of the model but a decisive confirmation of its theoretical scope: as an equity risk attribution framework, the Fama-French model should not---and empirically does not---explain the return dynamics of a commodity asset. Gold's returns are governed by macroeconomic forces entirely absent from the five-factor model, including real interest rates, inflation expectations, currency dynamics, and safe-haven demand. The strong and sustained performance of IAU documented in Figure~\ref{fig:cum_returns}, particularly from 2020 onward, combined with its statistical independence from all five equity factors, empirically validates its role as a genuine portfolio diversifier. For practitioners, this result reinforces the theoretical and empirical case for including non-correlated assets in a multi-asset portfolio: IAU's return-generating process is orthogonal to the systematic equity risk premiums that define the Fama-French framework, and its inclusion provides diversification benefits that cannot be replicated by any linear combination of the five factor portfolios.

\subsection{Limitations of the Analysis}

Several structural limitations warrant consideration when interpreting these results. First, while the use of USD-denominated tickers ensures direct alignment with the Fama-French factor portfolios constructed from U.S. equity data, this approach may not fully account for the foreign-currency dynamics and regional risk premia embedded in internationally-oriented ETFs such as VDEV.L, VWRA.L, and WSML.L, whose underlying holdings extend well beyond U.S. borders. Second, the portfolio of eight assets, while purposively selected to span a broad range of factor tilts, remains a small and non-random subset of the global ETF universe, and the regression results should not be generalized to other instruments. Third, the analysis period commencing in January 2015 encompasses structurally distinct macroeconomic regimes---including the post-quantitative-easing normalization cycle of 2017--2019, the COVID-19 market shock of 2020, and the subsequent large-cap technology boom of 2021--2026---each of which may have materially altered the realized magnitude and statistical significance of factor loadings. A rolling-window regression would provide deeper insight into the temporal stability of the estimated betas, but this lies beyond the current scope. Finally, the OLS framework rests on assumptions of homoskedastic and serially uncorrelated residuals. While the preliminary factor correlation analysis confirms the absence of severe multicollinearity among the five independent variables, formal tests for heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation were not conducted in this study and represent an avenue for methodological refinement in future work.
