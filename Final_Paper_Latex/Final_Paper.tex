\section{Introduction}
This document provides a rigorous evaluation of the \textbf{Fama-French 5-Factor Model} using R. The model extends the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) by incorporating Size, Value, Profitability, and Investment factors alongside standard market risk \parencite{fama1993common, fama2015five}. The core objective is to determine the efficacy of these five factors in explaining the excess returns of a portfolio comprising eight selected assets.
The shift from the single-factor CAPM was driven by its poor empirical record, which, as noted by \textcite{fama2004capital}, is often considered insufficient for practical applications. While the CAPM posits that risk is measured solely relative to a comprehensive market portfolio, empirical tests frequently show that the relation between average return and beta is flatter than predicted. This gap between theory and evidence necessitated a multidimensional approach to capture the risk factors that a single market beta could not account for.

\subsection{Transition from CAPM to Fama-French 5-Factor Model}
The Fama-French Model, developed by \textcite{fama1992cross} in their paper titled 'The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns', was originally composed of three (3) factors and serves as an augmentation to the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). The primary purpose of the introduction of this model was to demonstrate that market risk alone cannot fully explain stock returns. Thus, the transition from a single-factor (beta) linear model (CAPM) to a multi-factor linear model led to the creation of a model that could provide a multidimensional assessment for expected returns. Here, the CAPM model was expanded to include firm-specific variables that are historically known to have an effect on returns.

The initial Fama-French Model was composed of the following systematic risk factors. First, Market Risk (Rf), taken from the original CAPM model, represents the excess returns of the overall market over the overall risk-free rate. Next, SMB (small minus big) represents the idea that small-cap stocks tend to outperform large-cap stocks over time. Lastly, there is HML (high minus low) or the value factor; stocks with higher BM ratios tend to outperform stocks with low BM ratios. The three-factor model was originally developed to encapsulate the relationship between average return and price ratios (such as book-to-market ratio) \parencite{fama1993common}.

Moving forward, the Fama-French 3-Factor Model was further augmented by Fama and French in 2015 in their working paper titled 'A five-factor asset pricing model'. This model is composed of the three factors mentioned above with the further additions of RMW (robust minus weak) and CMA (conservative minus aggressive). The factor of RMW represents the difference between the returns of stocks with robust and weak profitability. Meanwhile, CMA represents the difference between the returns on diversified portfolios of stocks of low and high investment firms, which are referred to as conservative and aggressive \parencite{fama2015five}. These labels refer to a company's investment policy, whether it can be considered conservative or aggressive \parencite{MOSOEU202255}. This new model with these two newly added variables is what is now referred to as the Fama-French 5-Factor Model.

\subsection{Theoretical Significance and Professional Relevance}
The 5-factor model holds immense practical value for finance professionals, particularly in the realms of portfolio evaluation and cost of capital estimation. By identifying specific anomalies, such as the size and value effects, the model allows fund managers to more accurately assess whether a portfolio's "alpha" is the result of genuine skill or simply exposure to known risk premiums. 

Furthermore, the model's ability to provide a more nuanced risk assessment assists analysts in estimating the cost of equity more reliably than the traditional CAPM. Understanding these factors is vital for asset pricing, as it helps investors recognize that expected returns are tied not just to market volatility, but to the underlying characteristics of the firms themselves, such as their profitability and investment policies \parencite{fama2015five}.

\subsection{Impact on Risk, Pricing, and Portfolio Management}
The transition to a five-factor framework redefines systematic risk by incorporating profitability and investment policies—variables fundamentally linked to a firm's internal cash flows and reinvestment behavior \parencite{fama2015five}. By accounting for these additional dimensions, the model achieves superior pricing performance relative to the CAPM, frequently resulting in significantly lower regression intercepts (alphas). This enhanced explanatory power provides a robust benchmark for asset pricing and more reliable cost of equity estimations. Consequently, the model has profound implications for portfolio management: it enables precise performance attribution, allowing practitioners to distinguish between genuine managerial skill and returns derived from systematic factor tilts.

\subsection{Objectives}
The primary goal of this research is to evaluate the explanatory power of the Fama-French 5-Factor Model through the following specific objectives:
\begin{itemize}
    \item To conduct an empirical analysis assessing the efficacy of the five risk premiums, including Market, Size (SMB), Value (HML), Profitability (RMW), and Investment (CMA), in capturing the variations in returns across a diverse portfolio.
    \item To perform a statistical interpretation of the resulting alpha and beta coefficients, evaluating their significance and interpreting their economic implications for portfolio risk management.
    \item To develop a technical implementation of a fully reproducible R workflow that leverages the Kenneth French Data Library for factor ingestion and data alignment.
    \item To provide a theoretical justification and rigorous academic grounding for the selection of eight specific assets based on hypothesized factor exposures.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Research Design and Justification}
This study employs a quantitative research approach, utilizing an empirical case study design to evaluate the explanatory power of the Fama-French 5-Factor Model. The research design is cross-sectional and longitudinal, focusing on the statistical relationship between historical asset returns and established risk premiums over a defined temporal horizon. By using multivariate linear regression, the study aims to objectively measure factor loadings and assess their significance in a reproducible environment.

The choice of a multivariate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression framework is justified by asset pricing theory, which posits that the cross-section of expected returns can be explained by shared risk factors. This method is the most appropriate for this study because it allows for the decomposition of returns into specific risk premiums (Market, SMB, HML, RMW, and CMA), providing a granular view of an asset's risk profile that a single-factor model like the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) cannot achieve. Furthermore, using R and its specialized financial libraries ensures computational accuracy and the ability to handle high-frequency data transformations effectively.

\subsection{Technical Workflow and Data Integration}
The methodology follows a systematic, reproducible workflow designed to align historical asset returns with the Fama-French risk premiums. The process is divided into seven logical stages: environment setup, data ingestion, preprocessing, descriptive statistical profiling, modeling, visualization, and diagnostics. Each stage is designed to be fully automated, ensuring that the entire study can be replicated with a single execution of the source code. The complete technical implementation is provided in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_code}.

\subsubsection{Environment Setup and Dependencies}
To ensure the analysis runs smoothly, the first step involves preparing the computational environment. We utilize specialized R libraries for data science (\texttt{tidyverse}), financial analysis (\texttt{tidyquant}), and statistical reporting (\texttt{broom}). The implementation includes an automated routine that detects and installs any missing libraries, ensuring the project is "portable" across different computers. The setup logic is detailed in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_setup}.

\subsubsection{Data Acquisition and Automated Ingestion}
This stage handles the automated collection of data from two primary sources. First, we define a list of "tickers" representing our selected assets (e.g., VUSD.L for the S\&P 500, IAU for Gold). The script fetches historical price data for these assets from Yahoo Finance. Second, it loads the Five-Factor data---the independent variables we are testing---from the Kenneth French Data Library. To save time and avoid redundant network traffic, the code checks for local copies of these files before attempting to download them again. The ingestion script is provided in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_ingestion}.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing and Data Cleaning}
Before we can run our models, the raw data must be "cleaned" and synchronized. This involves three key tasks: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Return Calculation:} We convert daily prices into monthly "log-returns," which are mathematically better suited for linear regression.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Alignment:} Because different data sources use different date formats, we normalize all dates to the first of the month to ensure that asset returns are perfectly matched with their corresponding risk factors.
    \item \textbf{Excess Return Calculation:} We subtract the risk-free rate from our asset returns. This allows us to measure "Excess Return"---the specific portion of profit earned by taking market risk.
\end{enumerate}
The code for these transformations is documented in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_preprocessing}.

\subsubsection{Descriptive Statistical Profiling}
Prior to modeling, we compute summary statistics for each asset, including higher-moment metrics such as skewness and kurtosis. This profiling stage identifies non-normalities and outliers that may influence factor loadings. The implementation code is documented in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_stats}.

\subsubsection{Multivariate Regression Framework}
The core of our analysis is a multivariate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression. This is a statistical method that allows us to see how much each of the five factors (Market, Size, Value, Profitability, and Investment) contributes to an asset's total return. We use a modern "nest and map" programming approach, which allows us to run individual models for all eight assets simultaneously using the same logic. This ensures that our results are consistent across the entire portfolio. The modeling workflow is shown in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_modeling}.

\subsubsection{Visualization and High-Fidelity Plotting}
To make the statistical results easier to interpret, the workflow generates five types of high-fidelity charts. These include "Factor Loadings" (to show an asset's sensitivity to each factor) and "Cumulative Growth" (to show how a \$1 investment would have grown over time). We define a custom visual style called \texttt{theme\_quant} to ensure all graphics are professional and publication-ready. The visualization code is provided in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_viz}.

\subsubsection{Diagnostic Auditing}
The final step is a rigorous audit of our models. We calculate the $R^2$ (R-squared) value for each asset to see how well the model explains its returns. We also apply "significance stars" to our coefficients---a standard academic shorthand that tells the reader which results are statistically reliable and which might be due to random chance. These diagnostic routines are found in Appendix \ref{sec:appendix_diagnostics}.

This study utilizes a purposive sampling strategy. This exercise in selection isolates specific risk premiums from the malaise of the global market. The sample consists of eight exchange-traded funds. These assets represent specific tilts toward Market, Size, Value, Profitability, and Investment factors. London-listed UCITS variants optimize for regional tax efficiency. They provide direct exposure to U.S. systematic factors. \subsubsection{Low Explanatory Power of Gold as a Diagnostic Asset}

This outcome decisively confirms the diagnostic hypothesis that motivated its inclusion, as physical gold returns are structurally independent of the systematic equity risk factors. The stark contrast between IAU's near-zero model fit and the uniformly high explanatory power observed across the seven equity funds validates the model's correct specification, indicating that the framework captures equity-specific systematic risk instead of asset returns in general. As a further diagnostic, it empirically demonstrates that the strong Adjusted $R^2$ values obtained for the equity assets are not the product of overfitting or model misspecification, but rather reflect genuine systematic factor exposures.

These negative intercepts are most plausibly attributed to the real-world friction costs of investing in exchange-traded products, such as management expense ratios and transaction costs, which the theoretical Fama-French factor portfolios, being constructed as paper accounts, do not bear.  By contrast, VUSD.L exhibited an intercept indistinguishable from zero ($\hat{\alpha} = 0.0000$), while XLK's positive alpha of 0.0019 was also statistically insignificant, suggesting that for both funds, the estimated factor loadings fully account for observed returns. IAU's positive but statistically insignificant alpha ($\hat{\alpha} = 0.0035$, $p > 0.05$) further reinforces the interpretation that gold's return-generating process falls outside the explanatory domain of the five-factor equity model.

\subsection{Limitations of the Analysis}

Several structural limitations warrant consideration when interpreting these results. First, while the use of USD-denominated tickers ensures direct alignment with the Fama-French factor portfolios constructed from U.S. equity data, this approach may not fully account for the foreign-currency dynamics and regional risk premia embedded in internationally-oriented ETFs such as VDEV.L, VWRA.L, and WSML.L, whose underlying holdings extend well beyond U.S. borders. Second, the portfolio of eight assets, while purposively selected to span a broad range of factor tilts, remains a small and non-random subset of the global ETF universe, and the regression results should not be generalized to other instruments. Third, the analysis period commencing in January 2015 encompasses structurally distinct macroeconomic regimes, including the post-quantitative-easing normalization cycle of 2017--2019, the COVID-19 market shock of 2020, and the subsequent large-cap technology boom of 2021--2026, which may each have materially altered the realized magnitude and statistical significance of factor loadings.
 A rolling-window regression would provide deeper insight into the temporal stability of the estimated betas, but this lies beyond the current scope. Finally, the OLS framework rests on assumptions of homoskedastic and serially uncorrelated residuals. While the preliminary factor correlation analysis confirms the absence of severe multicollinearity among the five independent variables, formal tests for heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation were not conducted in this study and represent an avenue for methodological refinement in future work.

\section{Practical Implications for Portfolio Management}

\subsection{Alpha Interpretation for Asset Allocators}

The regression intercepts, specifically the alpha ($\hat{\alpha}$) coefficients, represent the portion of excess returns not attributable to any of the five systematic risk factors, and their magnitude and significance carry important economic implications. 

The intercept (alpha) represents the returns that cannot be explained by the factors in the Fama-French Model. Alphas can be interpreted differently for cost-of-capital estimation purposes. First, a zero alpha indicated that the model perfectly captures the required return for the asset. Meanwhile, a positive alpha indicates that the returns are above the required cost of capital. Meanwhile, as a measure of manager selection, alpha is used as a measure of a manager’s skill. If a manager receives consistently positive alphas, it means that the manager generates positive returns beyond what can be explained by systematic risk exposure.


The negative alpha of QUAL (0.0029) suggests that QUAL underperforms the return predicted by the Fama-French 5-Factor Model by approximately 0.29 percent per period. QUAL generates abnormal negative returns. This indicates that even accounting for market risk, firm size, value, profitability, and investment factors, the asset delivers lower expected returns. These abnormal negative returns may potentially be a result of additional expenses such as management fees and transaction costs. They reduce the net returns received by investors and can potentially be the reason behind the negative returns. Moving forward, the negative alpha of VWRA.L (0.0030) indicates that the asset’s realized returns are 0.30 percent lower than predicted. Considering that it is a global-index tracking ETF, it incurs trading and tracking costs (as a result of replicating the underlying index) that are not captured by the model but are incurred by investors. This may result in the negative abnormal return as seen in the alpha.

The process of decomposing returns is the process of breaking down an asset's (or portfolio’s) total returns into components that explain where the return came from.  By estimating each asset’s sensitivity (beta) to the market, size, value, profitability, and investment factors, the model attributes most of the asset’s return variation to systematic sources of risk. Any remaining unexplained return is captured by alpha. This process can help practitioners distinguish returns produced by managerial skill from returns generated from systemic risk factors. Thus, if returns are mostly explained by exposures such as those present in the Fama-French model (market risk, size, value, profitability, and investment), then asset or portfolio performance can be attributed to factor tilts rather than the ability of managers.

The Fama-French model is a further expansion of the CAPM model. It serves as a more rigorous benchmark for asset or portfolio performance, as it accounts for multiple systemic drivers of returns rather than only relying on market risk as a factor (CAPM). This allows practitioners to have a more accurate and all-encompassing evaluation of portfolio performance, estimate the cost of capital, and assess whether the excess returns are a result of managerial skill or exposure to known risk factors.


\subsection{Factor Timing and the Divergence Between Exposure and Reward}

The Small Minus Big (SMB) factor, which is frequently used when determining the probability of a small-cap premium, shows the return difference between companies with small and high capitalizations. The Fama-French Five-Factor Model's regression results highlight that the majority of the portfolio's assets exhibit minimal exposure to the SMB factor. Most ETFs, such as VUSD.L (-0.103), VDEV.L (-0.065), QUAL (-0.092), VIG (-0.056), XLK (-0.254), and IAU (-0.089), have negative SMB coefficients, indicating that their return behavior is more in line with large-cap investments than small-cap companies. The sole specialized small-cap ETF that has an elevated positive SMB loading (0.522) with significant statistical significance (p < 0.001) is WSML.L, which effectively introduces investors to size factors. The SMB coefficient for the worldwide market benchmark VWRA.L is nearly nonexistent or close to zero (0.005), suggesting little susceptibility to the small-cap premium. These results imply that the returns of the majority of assets throughout the sample period were mostly explained by the small-cap factor.

Descriptive statistics adhere to the interpretation of small-cap premium compression from 2015 to 2026. The mean return (0.0159) of the small-cap ETF WSML.L was not significantly higher in comparison of large-cap or broad-market ETFs like VUSD.L (0.0162) and VWRA.L (0.0194), despite the fact that it showed the highest volatility amongst the equity assets (SD = 0.0543). In real terms, despite similar or lower risk levels, some large-cap-oriented assets, such XLK and VWRA.L, produced better average returns. This pattern suggests that during the study period, investors did not consistently obtain an additional return for holding smaller enterprises. Large-capitalization stocks, especially those connected to technology and international market benchmarks, seem to have been the main drivers to achieve success instead. As a result, the empirical findings support the theory of small-cap premium compression, which holds that in the current market situation, small-cap companies' historical advantage over large-cap stocks has decreased.

\subsection{Risk-Aware Diversification with Non-Equity Assets}

The statistical results for IAU (Gold) provide a solid justification for the inclusion of non-equity assets within a diversified portfolio. The Fama-French model has extremely limited explanatory power when applied to IAU. As shown in Table 3, IAU only has an adjusted R-squared value of 0.068 and a p-value of 0.102 (making IAU not statistically significant). Thus, this indicates that the five equity factors fail to explain the variability in IAU returns. This further proves that the dynamics of gold as an asset run independently from the systemic equity risk factors captured by the framework. 

Considering that gold behaves differently and independently from traditional equity assets, it functions as a portfolio diversifier. While equity returns are largely exposed to market, firm size, value, profitability, and investment policies, gold’s performance is primarily driven by macroeconomic factors such as inflation, interest rates, its function as a safe-haven asset, and other factors (Hayes, 2025). These drivers are beyond the scope of the Fama-French model, and the returns of gold cannot be replicated by any combination of equity factor exposures.

During periods of significant economic stress, the presence of an asset whose dynamics are uncorrelated with equity risk factors may provide significant risk aversion benefits. Non-equity assets such as gold, as mentioned previously, can provide portfolio stability in times of economic drawdowns. Thus, including gold may allow a portfolio to weather harsh economic conditions, as the macroeconomic factors that affect them (real interest rates, inflation, political factors, etc.) are distinct from factors that affect equities. (Why gold is a good anchor for modern portfolios, 2026). 

In this instance, IAU (gold) has demonstrated statistical independence from Fama-French equity factors. Thus, this shows that it is driven by other macroeconomic forces such as inflation, real interest rates, and safe-haven demand (among others). Considering this, allocating a portion of your resources towards a non-correlated asset such as gold can reduce overall portfolio volatility and mitigate losses during periods of equity market downturns. As a result, portfolio resilience and risk-adjusted performance are improved.



\section{Conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of Empirical Findings}

The results show that the assets largely behaved in line with their expected factor tilts, although the strength of these relationships varies across the portfolio. WSML.L clearly captures the size effect, with a strong and statistically significant positive SMB loading. However, despite this exposure, its relatively weak performance over the sample period confirms that carrying a factor does not guarantee being rewarded for it. This reflects the well-documented compression of the small-cap premium from 2015 to 2026.
On the other hand, QUAL and VIG strongly validate the profitability (RMW) factor. Both exhibit significant positive loadings and deliver relatively strong and stable returns, reinforcing the idea that profitability remains a reliable driver of excess returns within equity markets.
XLK stands out as the most aggressive asset in the portfolio. Its high market beta and significantly negative HML loading confirm its strong growth orientation, while its superior realized returns reflect the dominance of the technology sector, especially in the post-2020 period.
For the international funds (VDEV.L, VWRA.L, and VUSD.L), the model still performs well in terms of explanatory power. However, their factor interpretations are less clean due to broader geographic exposure and differences in investment structure. The presence of positive CMA loadings for some of these funds suggests a tilt toward more conservative investment behavior, though this is not as sharply defined as in the U.S.-focused assets.
Finally, IAU behaves exactly as expected—it shows no meaningful sensitivity to any of the five equity factors. Its low explanatory power and distinct return profile confirm that gold operates outside the equity factor framework, reinforcing its role as a diversification asset rather than a return driver within this model.

\subsection{Model Efficacy Verdict}

Overall, the Fama-French 5-Factor Model performs extremely well for equity assets but does not extend effectively to a broader multi-asset setting. For the equity core of the portfolio, the model is clearly sufficient. All seven equity funds show very high Adjusted R2 values and statistically significant results, indicating that the model captures most of the variation in returns. The fact that alpha is largely insignificant across these assets further supports this, suggesting that returns are primarily driven by systematic factors rather than unexplained excess performance.
However, the model breaks down when applied outside equities. The case of IAU makes this very clear—its low Adjusted R2 and insignificant model results show that the five-factor framework simply does not apply to assets like gold.Because of this, the model should be seen as highly effective within its intended scope, but not as a universal framework for all asset classes.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Research Directions}

While the model performs strongly, there are several limitations that need to be acknowledged.
First, the factors used in the model are based on U.S. data, which creates a mismatch when applied to international ETFs like VDEV.L, VWRA.L, and WSML.L. Even with USD alignment, these assets are still influenced by regional economic conditions that the model does not fully capture.
Second, factor performance is not stable over time. The sample period includes multiple major economic phases, such as the COVID-19 shock and the more recent monetary tightening cycle. These shifts likely affected how factors behaved, particularly in the case of the weakened size premium and the strong performance of growth-oriented assets.
Third, the analysis relies on standard OLS assumptions but does not formally test for issues like heteroskedasticity or autocorrelation. This means that while the results are statistically strong, there may still be underlying estimation limitations.
Future research could address these gaps by incorporating international factor models, applying rolling regressions to track how exposures change over time, and exploring more flexible approaches such as Bayesian estimation. Expanding the dataset and including macroeconomic variables would also provide a more complete view of asset return behavior.

\subsection{Final Academic Verdict} 

The Fama-French 5-Factor Model remains a highly effective tool in modern quantitative finance, particularly when applied to equity portfolios. In this study, it demonstrates strong explanatory power and provides clear insight into how different assets derive their returns. The alignment between factor loadings and each fund’s investment strategy further confirms its usefulness as a risk attribution framework. At the same time, the results highlight an important limitation: the model is not meant to explain all assets. Its failure to account for gold returns is not a weakness, but rather a reminder that it is specifically designed for equities. Overall, the model is best understood as a powerful but specialized tool—highly reliable for analyzing diversified equity portfolios, but requiring adaptation when extended to global or multi-asset contexts.

        df_tidy <- as.data.frame(na.omit(df)) %>%
          rownames_to_column(var = "Date") %>%
          as_tibble()
        write_csv(df_tidy, file_path)
        message(paste("Downloaded:", symbol))
      }
    }, error = function(e) message(paste("Failed to download", symbol, ":", e$message)))
  } else {
    message(paste("Using existing local data for:", symbol))
  }
})

# 3. Monthly Return Calculation Function
load_asset_returns <- function(ticker) {
  file_path <- paste0("data/", ticker, ".csv")
  if (!file.exists(file_path)) return(NULL)
  
  df <- read_csv(file_path, show_col_types = FALSE) %>%
    mutate(Date = as.Date(Date)) %>%
    arrange(Date)
  
  adj_col <- grep("Adjusted", names(df), value = TRUE)
  if (length(adj_col) == 0) return(NULL)
  
  xts_df <- xts(df[[adj_col]], order.by = df$Date)
  monthly_rets <- periodReturn(xts_df, period = "monthly", type = "log")
  
  df_ret <- as.data.frame(monthly_rets) %>%
    rownames_to_column(var = "Date") %>%
    mutate(Date = as.Date(Date)) %>%
    rename(!!ticker := monthly.returns)
  
  return(df_ret)
}
